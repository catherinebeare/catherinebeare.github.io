Binomials can be a tricky topic. Binomials are a fundamental topic in many pure mathematics topics. This page aims to help you with your understanding of binomials.

What is a Binomial?

A binomial is an algebraic expression that contains two different terms connected by addition or subtraction.

Which of the following is a binomial expression?

\( 3x^2 \) consists of one term, and is not connected by addition or subtraction (this is called a monomial). \( 2x^3 + 3y^5 - 1 \) consists of 3 terms (this is an example of a trinomial). \( 4x + 7 \) is an algebraic expression containing two different terms which are connected by addition.

First, we need to understand some notation.

Factorial!

What is factorial? Factorial (denoted "!") is the product of an integer and all the integers below it. For example,

\[ 5! = 5 \times 4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1 = 120 \]

Note: by definition, \(0! = 1 \).

What is the value of 4!?

\(4! = 4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1 = 24.\)

Submit

Due to the nature of factorials, we can often cancel before dividing. For example,

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{7!}{4!} &= \frac{7 \times 6 \times 5 \times \cancel{4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1}}{\cancel{4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1}} \\ &= 7 \times 6 \times 5 \\ &= 210 \\ \end{aligned} \]

We will see this cancelling in action when we look at combinatorial notation.

\( \begin{aligned} \text{Now, let } n &= \text{the total number of events} \\ \text{and } k &= \text{the number of desired outcomes.} \\ \end{aligned} \)

Then we can say the number of ways to get a specific outcome is “n choose k”. We write \[\binom{n}{k}=\frac{n!}{k!\left(n-k\right)!}.\] These values can be calculated using the "\(^nC_r\)" (combinatorial) button on your calulator.

These are the coefficients in a binomial expansion.

How many different ways are there to flip a coin six times and get heads four of those times?

The number of events is the total number of coin tosses, giving us \(n=6\). We also need the number of times we want to get heads (our specific outcome), so we have \(k=4\). Therefore, there are \(\binom{6}{4} = 15 \) ways to get four heads from six coin tosses.

In combinatorial notation, \[ ^6C_4 = \frac{6!}{(6-4)!4!} = \frac{6 \times 5 \times \cancel{4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1}}{(2 \times 1) \times \cancel{4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1}} = \frac{30}{2} = 15.\]

We have seen how the binomial coefficients can be calculated and what they represent. These coefficients can be represented in Pascal's triangle.

Pascal's Triangle

To construct Pascal's triangle, we begin by placing a 1 in the first row (we call this row 0). To calculate the next row, we use numbers in the subsequent rows. We add the two numbers directly above the number we need to calculate. If there are no numbers to the left or right hand side, we place a zero for that side and proceed with the addition (this means the outer diagonals of the triangle will be 1s).

Binomial Expansion

Positive Integer Powers

We know that a binomial expression contains two terms, such as \((1+x)\). If we want to square this bracket, we can easily expand it. However, what if we want to calculate \((1+x)^8\)? This would be extremely time consuming. This is where the binomial expansion comes in useful.

\( \text{The Binomial theorem is given by} \) \[(x+a)^n = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k}x^k a^{n-k},\]
\( \begin{aligned} \text{where } n &= \ \text{the number of trials,} \\ k &= \ \text{the number of successes}, \\ x &= \ \text{the probability of success on a single trial,} \\ a &= \ \text{the probability of failure on a single trial.} \\ \end{aligned} \)

Example 1: Write down the binomial expansion for \( (1 + x)^4 \).

\[ \begin{aligned} (1 + x)^4 &= \binom{4}{0} 1 + \binom{4}{1} x + \binom{4}{2} x^2 + \binom{4}{3} x^3 + \binom{4}{4} x^4 \\ &= 1 + 4x + 6x^2 + 4x^3 + x^4 \\ \end{aligned} \]

Notice that the coefficients are row 4 from Pascal's triangle, as the binomial is raised to the power of 4.

Example 2: Find the binomial expansion for \((1+2x)^3\).

\[ \begin{aligned} (1 + 2x)^3 &= \binom{3}{0} 1 + \binom{3}{1} (2x) + \binom{3}{2} (2x)^2 + \binom{3}{3} (2x)^3 \\ &= 1 + 3(2x) + 3(2x)^2 + (2x)^3 \\ &= 1 + 6x + 12x^2 + 8x^3 \\ \end{aligned} \]

Example 3: Expand \((2+x)^5\).

\[ \begin{aligned} (2 + x)^5 &= \binom{5}{0} 2^5 + \binom{5}{1} x \times 2^4 + \binom{5}{2} x^2 \times 2^3 + \binom{5}{3} x^3 \times 2^2 + \binom{5}{4} x^4 \times 2 + \binom{5}{5} x^5 \\ &= 32 + 80x + 80x^2 + 40x^3 + 10x^4 + x^5 \\ \end{aligned}\]

What happens when our terms are connected by subtraction? We use the same formula, taking care with negative signs. This is easiest to see with an example.

Example 4: Expand \((x-7)^4\).

\[ \begin{aligned} (x-7)^4 &= \binom{4}{0} x^4 + \binom{4}{1} x^3 \times -7 + \binom{4}{2} x^2 \times (-7)^2+ \binom{4}{3} x \times (-7)^3 + \binom{4}{4} (-7)^4 \\ &= x^4 - 28 x^3 + 294 x^2 - 1372 x + 2401 \\ \end{aligned} \]

Negative Integer Powers

When \(n\) is a negative integer, the expansion becomes an infinite series. As an example, let's consider the expansion of \((1+x)^{-n}\), where \(n\) is a positive integer:

\((1+x)^{-n} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \binom{-n}{k} 1^{-n-k} x^k\)

Using the binomial coefficient formula, we can rewrite the series as:

\((1+x)^{-n} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-n)(-n-1)(-n-2)...(-n-k+1)}{k!} x^k\)

This infinite series converges if \(|x| < 1\), and its expansion can be used to compute the binomial expression with negative integer powers.
